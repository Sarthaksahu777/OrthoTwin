5. Is the surgery engine truly geometry-first?
Partially. Implant, spacer, and alignment correction start with geometry changes. Fusion is relationship/constraint-first. Rebuilt metrics are generated after anatomy edits, but several major changes are caused by deterministic rebuild formulas and method changes rather than real anatomical geometry alone.
Implant, spacer, and alignment correction are geometry-first in the limited object/centroid model. Fusion is not fully geometry-first because it records no geometry_changes; it adds a fusion object, constraints, and a graph edge.

6. Which outputs are directly derived from geometry?
Directly geometry-derived: geometry_changes, changed centroids, changed disc height, relative_spacing, rebuilt slippage from centroid projection, rebuilt curvature from centroid angles, rebuilt alignment from centroid vectors. Visual before/after plots are also centroid-derived.

Brutal honesty:
- The Phase 7 architecture is a real improvement over Phase 5/6 because anatomy objects and centroids are modified before downstream metrics are rebuilt.
- It is still not a true anatomical/biomechanical surgery simulator: no mesh deformation, no real implant geometry, no finite element model, no tissue mechanics, no calibrated physical spacing, and no clinical validation.
- The largest reported curvature/alignment differences are partly artifacts of changing measurement methodology during rebuild, not necessarily consequences of surgery.
- Fusion is the weakest geometry-first case because it does not move anatomy or alter disc geometry; it mainly changes constraints and graph relationships.
